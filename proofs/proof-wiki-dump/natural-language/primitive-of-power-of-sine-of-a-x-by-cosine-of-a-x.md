Theorem
$\ds \int \sin^n a x \cos a x \rd x = \frac {\sin^{n + 1} a x} {\paren {n + 1} a} + C$
for $n \ne -1$.


Proof













\(\ds z\)

\(=\)







\(\ds \sin a x\)














\(\ds \leadsto \ \ \)





\(\ds \frac {\d z} {\d x}\)

\(=\)







\(\ds a \cos a x\)





Primitive of $\sin a x$








\(\ds \leadsto \ \ \)





\(\ds \int \sin^n a x \cos a x \rd x\)

\(=\)







\(\ds \int \frac {z^n \rd x} a\)





Integration by Substitution














\(\ds \)

\(=\)







\(\ds \frac {z^{n + 1} } {\paren {n + 1} a} + C\)





Primitive of Power














\(\ds \)

\(=\)







\(\ds \frac {\sin^{n + 1} a x} {\paren {n + 1} a} + C\)





substituting for $z$



$\blacksquare$


Also see
Primitive of $\cos^n a x \sin a x$
For $n = -1$, use Primitive of $\dfrac {\cos a x} {\sin a x}$.
