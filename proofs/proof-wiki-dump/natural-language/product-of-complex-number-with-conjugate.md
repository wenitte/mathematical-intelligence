Theorem
Let $z = a + i b \in \C$ be a complex number.
Let $\overline z$ denote the complex conjugate of $z$.

Then:

$z \overline z = a^2 + b^2 = \cmod z^2$
and thus is wholly real.


Proof
By the definition of a complex number, let $z = a + i b$ where $a$ and $b$ are real numbers.
Then:














\(\ds z \overline z\)

\(=\)







\(\ds \paren {a + i b} \paren {a - i b}\)





Definition of Complex Conjugate














\(\ds \)

\(=\)







\(\ds a^2 + a \cdot i b + a \cdot \paren {-i b} + i \cdot \paren {-i} \cdot b^2\)





Definition of Complex Multiplication














\(\ds \)

\(=\)







\(\ds a^2 + i a b - i a b + b^2\)




















\(\ds \)

\(=\)







\(\ds a^2 + b^2\)




















\(\ds \)

\(=\)







\(\ds \paren {\sqrt {a^2 + b^2} }^2\)




















\(\ds \)

\(=\)







\(\ds \cmod z^2\)





Definition of Complex Modulus



As $a^2 + b^2$ is wholly real, the result follows.
$\blacksquare$
