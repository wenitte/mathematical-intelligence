Theorem
Let $r \in \R_{> 0}$ be a positive real number.
Let $n \in \Z$ be an integer.
Let $r^n$ be defined as $r$ to the power of $n$.

Then:

$r^{-n} = \dfrac 1 {r^n}$


Proof
Let $n \in \Z_{\ge 0}$.
Then from Real Number to Negative Power: Positive Integer:

$r^{-n} = \dfrac 1 {r^n}$
It remains to show that this holds when $n < 0$.
Let $n \in \Z_{<0}$.
Then $n = - m$ for some $m \in \Z_{> 0}$.
Thus:














\(\ds r^{-m}\)

\(=\)







\(\ds \dfrac 1 {r^m}\)





Real Number to Negative Power: Positive Integer








\(\ds \leadsto \ \ \)





\(\ds \dfrac 1 {r^{-m} }\)

\(=\)







\(\ds \dfrac 1 {1 / r^m}\)





taking reciprocal of both sides














\(\ds \)

\(=\)







\(\ds r^m\)














\(\ds \leadsto \ \ \)





\(\ds \dfrac 1 {r^n}\)

\(=\)







\(\ds r^{-n}\)









$\blacksquare$
