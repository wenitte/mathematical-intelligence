Theorem
Let $\RR$ be a relation on a set $S$.

If $\RR$ is non-reflexive, then so is $\RR^{-1}$.


Proof
Let $\RR$ be non-reflexive.

Then:










\(\ds \exists x \in S: \, \)



\(\ds \tuple {x, x}\)

\(\in\)







\(\ds \RR\)





as $\RR$ is not antireflexive








\(\ds \leadsto \ \ \)

\(\ds \exists x \in S: \, \)



\(\ds \tuple {x, x}\)

\(\in\)







\(\ds \RR^{-1}\)





Definition of Inverse Relation



Thus $\RR^{-1}$ is not antireflexive.

Also:










\(\ds \exists x \in S: \, \)



\(\ds \tuple {x, x}\)

\(\notin\)







\(\ds \RR\)





as $\RR$ is not reflexive








\(\ds \leadsto \ \ \)

\(\ds \exists x \in S: \, \)



\(\ds \tuple {x, x}\)

\(\notin\)







\(\ds \RR^{-1}\)





Definition of Inverse Relation



Thus $\RR^{-1}$ is not reflexive.

Hence the result, by definition of non-reflexive relation.
$\blacksquare$
