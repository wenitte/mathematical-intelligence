Lemma for Primitive of $\dfrac 1 {\paren {p + q \sin a x}^2}$
Let $u = \tan \dfrac \theta 2$.
Then:

$\dfrac 1 {p + q \sin a x} = \dfrac {u^2 + 1} {p u^2 + 2 q u + p}$


Proof
From Tangent Half-Angle Substitution for Sine:














\(\ds u\)

\(=\)







\(\ds \tan \dfrac \theta 2\)














\(\ds \leadsto \ \ \)





\(\ds \sin \theta\)

\(=\)







\(\ds \dfrac {2 u} {u^2 + 1}\)














\(\ds \leadsto \ \ \)





\(\ds \dfrac 1 {p + q \sin a x}\)

\(=\)







\(\ds \dfrac 1 {p + q \paren {\dfrac {2 u} {u^2 + 1} } }\)




















\(\ds \)

\(=\)







\(\ds \dfrac 1 {\frac {p \paren {u^2 + 1} + 2 q u} {u^2 + 1} }\)




















\(\ds \)

\(=\)







\(\ds \dfrac {u^2 + 1} {p u^2 + 2 q u + p}\)









$\blacksquare$
