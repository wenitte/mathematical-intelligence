Example of Use of Division of Complex Numbers in Polar Form
$\dfrac {\paren {8 \cis 40 \degrees}^3} {\paren {2 \cis 60 \degrees}^4} = -16 - 16 \sqrt 3 i$


Proof













\(\ds \dfrac {\paren {8 \cis 40 \degrees}^3} {\paren {2 \cis 60 \degrees}^4}\)

\(=\)







\(\ds \dfrac {8^3 \paren {\map \cis {3 \times 40 \degrees} } } {2^4 \paren {\map \cis {4 \times 60 \degrees} } }\)





De Moivre's Theorem














\(\ds \)

\(=\)







\(\ds \dfrac {2^9} {2^4} \map \cis {120 \degrees - 240 \degrees}\)





Division of Complex Numbers in Polar Form














\(\ds \)

\(=\)







\(\ds 2^5 \paren {\map \cos {-120 \degrees} + i \, \map \sin {-120 \degrees} }\)




















\(\ds \)

\(=\)







\(\ds 32 \times \paren {-\dfrac 1 2} + 32 i \paren {-\dfrac {\sqrt 3} 2}\)





Cosine of $240 \degrees$, Sine of $240 \degrees$














\(\ds \)

\(=\)







\(\ds -16 - 16 \sqrt 3 i\)





simplifying



$\blacksquare$
